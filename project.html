Joseph Smith Jr. (1805–1844) is the founder and first prophet of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. This page summarizes his life, key events in the Restoration, and central teachings presented in a neutral, factual style appropriate for lessons or study guides.

Early Life

Joseph Smith was born on December 23, 1805, in Sharon, Vermont. Raised in a poor but religious family, he grew up during the American religious revival known as the Second Great Awakening. As a young teen he prayed to know which church was true—an experience that led to a foundational event in his life.

The First Vision (c. 1820)

Joseph described a vision in which God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to him. That event — commonly called the "First Vision" — is understood by Latter-day Saints as the beginning of the Restoration of the gospel.

"I saw a pillar of light... When the light rested upon me I saw two Personages... one of them spake unto me, calling me by name... This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!"

The Restoration & The Book of Mormon

Following the First Vision, Joseph reported further visitations and was given the golden plates, from which he translated the Book of Mormon (published 1830). In the early 1830s he organized the Church of Christ (later named The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints) and taught that priesthood authority and temple ordinances were restored to the earth.

Key Teachings

  • The Nature of God: God the Father and Jesus Christ are distinct, embodied beings who communicate with humanity.
  • Continuing Revelation: Modern prophets receive revelation for the Church; personal revelation is encouraged.
  • Plan of Salvation: Emphasis on pre-mortal existence, earthly probation, Atonement, resurrection, and the potential for exaltation.
  • Eternal Families: Through temple ordinances, families can be sealed for time and all eternity.
  • Scriptural Canon: Scripture includes the Bible, the Book of Mormon, Doctrine and Covenants, and the Pearl of Great Price (for those in the LDS tradition).

Persecution & Martyrdom

Joseph Smith and early church members faced legal challenges, violence, and persecution in several states. On June 27, 1844, Joseph and his brother Hyrum were killed at Carthage Jail in Illinois. His death had a profound impact on the movement he led.

Legacy

Joseph Smith's influence extends into theology, church organization, scripture, and community life. Millions read the Book of Mormon and follow traditions that grew from his teachings. Historians assess his life from multiple viewpoints; this page focuses on a clear, neutral summary useful for study and teaching.

Notable Quotes

  • "If men do not comprehend the character of God, they do not comprehend themselves."
  • "I told the brethren that the Book of Mormon was the most correct of any book on earth..."
  • "I am calm as a summer’s morning. I have a conscience void of offense toward God and toward all men." (reported near his death)
